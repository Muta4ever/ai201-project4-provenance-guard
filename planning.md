# Provenance Guard — Planning & Specification

This document is written **before** implementation. It is the contract every module
implements against, and the reference I hand to AI tools when generating code.

---

## Problem framing

Creative platforms need to tell readers whether a piece of text was written by a human
or generated by AI — **not to police creativity, but to protect attribution and give
readers context**. Perfect AI detection is unsolved, so the real engineering challenge
is to *communicate uncertainty honestly* and give creators a path to appeal.

**Design principle that drives every threshold below:** on a writing platform, a false
positive (labeling a human's work as AI) is worse than a false negative. So the system is
deliberately *reluctant* to call something AI — it needs strong, agreeing signals before
it does, and it defaults to "uncertain" whenever the evidence is mixed.

---

## Architecture

### Component narrative

A submission enters through `POST /submit`. The raw text is fanned out to the detection
pipeline, which runs three independent signals. Their per-signal AI-likelihood scores are
combined (weighted, with a disagreement penalty) into a single calibrated `confidence`
score in `[0, 1]`. That score is mapped to one of three attribution verdicts and a matching
transparency label. The result — plus every signal score — is persisted to the content
store and written to the structured audit log, then returned to the caller.

An appeal enters through `POST /appeal` with the `content_id` from the original decision.
The system flips that content's status to `under_review`, writes an `appeal` event to the
audit log (linked to the original classification and carrying the creator's reasoning), and
returns a confirmation. No automated re-classification happens — the appeal simply enters a
human review queue, surfaced via `GET /log`.

## 1. Detection signals

The pipeline uses **three** distinct signals (two are required; the third earns the
*ensemble* stretch). "Distinct" means they measure genuinely different properties of the
text: one semantic, one structural, one lexical.

Every signal returns the same shape:
`{ "ai_likelihood": float in [0,1], "reliable": bool, "detail": {...} }`
where `ai_likelihood = 1.0` means "looks fully AI" and `0.0` means "looks fully human".

### Signal 1 — LLM classifier (Groq `llama-3.3-70b-versatile`)  · weight 0.50
- **Measures:** holistic semantic and stylistic coherence — does the passage *read* like
  machine text (even, hedged, generically fluent) or like a specific human voice?
- **Why it differs:** AI text tends to be globally coherent but locally generic, with
  balanced hedging and low idiosyncrasy. An LLM is good at this gestalt judgment that no
  single statistic captures.
- **Output:** the model is prompted to return JSON `{ai_probability, reasoning}`; we take
  `ai_probability` as `ai_likelihood`.
- **Blind spot:** LLM detectors are known to be biased against **non-native English
  writers** and highly formal human prose (flagging them as AI), have no ground truth, and
  can be fooled by lightly paraphrased AI text. This is exactly the false-positive risk we
  must guard against — so it never decides alone.

### Signal 2 — Structural stylometry (burstiness)  · weight 0.25
- **Measures:** the *rhythm* of the text — coefficient of variation of sentence length,
  plus how uniform sentence lengths are around the mean.
- **Why it differs:** human writing is "bursty" — a mix of long and short sentences. AI
  writing is more uniform in sentence length. Low variance → more AI-like.
- **Output:** `ai_likelihood` blended from burstiness (60%) and uniformity (40%).
- **Blind spot:** unreliable on **short texts** (few sentences → meaningless variance) and
  misfires on intentionally uniform human forms — **poetry, song lyrics, lists, aphorisms**.
  Marked `reliable=false` when the text has fewer than ~4 sentences / 25 words.

### Signal 3 — Lexical / register  · weight 0.25
- **Measures:** vocabulary register — density of informal markers (contractions, filler,
  lowercase "i", slang) vs. AI-favored connective/register words ("furthermore", "moreover",
  "it is important to note", "delve", "leverage", "paradigm").
- **Why it differs:** casual human writing carries informal markers; AI defaults to a
  polished, connective-heavy register.
- **Output:** `ai_likelihood` blended from informality (low informality → AI) and
  AI-transition-word density.
- **Blind spot:** **formal human writing** (academic, legal, financial) has no informal
  markers and uses connective words, so it scores AI-like here. Deliberately given low weight
  and cross-checked by the LLM signal for this reason.

## 2. Uncertainty representation

**What `confidence` means:** it is the pipeline's combined estimate of `P(text is AI)`,
in `[0, 1]`.
- `0.50` = maximum uncertainty (a coin flip — the system refuses to make a call).
- Toward `1.0` = increasingly confident the text is **AI**.
- Toward `0.0` = increasingly confident the text is **human**.

So `0.51` sits in the uncertain band and produces a *materially different* label than
`0.95` (high-confidence AI) — the score is not a binary flip at 0.5.

**Combining raw signals into a calibrated score:**

```
stylometry = 0.5 * structural + 0.5 * lexical
raw        = 0.50 * llm + 0.25 * structural + 0.25 * lexical
           = 0.50 * llm + 0.50 * stylometry

# Disagreement penalty — pull toward 0.5 when the semantic signal (LLM)
# and the statistical signals (stylometry) disagree. Mixed evidence => uncertain.
d          = abs(llm - stylometry)                 # 0..1
confidence = raw - (raw - 0.5) * d * 0.5           # dampen toward 0.5
```

If the structural signal is `reliable=false` (short text), its weight is redistributed to
the LLM signal, and the final confidence is clamped toward the uncertain band so we never
make a high-confidence call on too little evidence.

**Thresholds (asymmetric, biased against false positives):**

| Combined confidence `c` | Attribution     | Label variant           |
|-------------------------|-----------------|-------------------------|
| `c >= 0.70`             | `likely_ai`     | high-confidence AI      |
| `0.30 < c < 0.70`       | `uncertain`     | uncertain               |
| `c <= 0.30`             | `likely_human`  | high-confidence human   |

The AI threshold (0.70) is intentionally high and the uncertain band is wide (0.30–0.70):
we only call something AI when the evidence is strong, because wrongly accusing a human is
the costliest error.

---

## 3. Transparency label design

Three variants. Each shows a plain-language headline, a body a non-technical reader
understands, and a confidence line. **Exact text (verbatim):**

**High-confidence AI** (`c >= 0.70`)
> **⚠ Likely AI-generated**
> Our analysis suggests this content was most likely produced with the help of a
> generative AI tool. This is an automated estimate, not a certainty.
> Confidence: {pct}% likely AI-generated. The creator can contest this result.

**High-confidence human** (`c <= 0.30`)
> **✓ Likely human-written**
> Our analysis found no strong signals of AI generation — this reads as human-written.
> This is an automated estimate, not a guarantee of authorship.
> Confidence: {pct}% likely human-written.

**Uncertain** (`0.30 < c < 0.70`)
> **◑ Origin uncertain**
> Our analysis was inconclusive. We can't reliably tell whether this was written by a
> person or generated by AI, so we are not making a determination.
> Treat the origin as unknown. The creator can add context.

`{pct}` is derived from the distance of `c` from 0.5 so the number always reads in the
label's direction (e.g. `c=0.05` → "95% likely human-written").

## 4. Appeals workflow

- **Who:** the creator of a classified piece of content (identified by `content_id`).
- **What they provide:** `content_id` + `creator_reasoning` (free text explaining why they
  believe the classification is wrong — e.g. "I'm a non-native speaker, my formal style is
  human").
- **What the system does on receipt:**
  1. Looks up the original decision by `content_id` (404 if unknown).
  2. Updates the content's status to `under_review` in the content store.
  3. Writes an `appeal` event to the audit log, carrying the original attribution/confidence/
     signal scores **and** the creator's reasoning, so the full history sits in one place.
  4. Returns a confirmation (content_id, new status, timestamp). No automated
     re-classification.
- **What a human reviewer sees:** `GET /log` returns the interleaved history — the original
  `classified` entry with all signal scores next to the `appeal` entry with the creator's
  reasoning — enough to make an informed manual decision.

## 5. Anticipated edge cases

1. **Formal human writing (academic / financial / legal).** No contractions or slang, heavy
   use of connective words, uniform sentence structure → the lexical and structural signals
   both push toward AI. This is the classic false positive. *Mitigation:* LLM carries the
   most weight, the disagreement penalty pulls mixed cases toward uncertain, and the AI
   threshold is high (0.70). The example "monetary policy" paragraph should land in the
   uncertain band, not "likely AI".

2. **Short or structurally uniform creative forms (poetry, lyrics, aphorisms, lists).**
   Deliberate repetition and uniform line length make burstiness look AI-like, and there are
   too few sentences for reliable statistics. *Mitigation:* the structural signal reports
   `reliable=false` below ~4 sentences / 25 words; its weight is redistributed and the final
   score is clamped toward uncertain so we don't confidently mislabel a short poem.

3. **Lightly-edited AI output.** A human tweaks AI text to add a couple of contractions and
   a short sentence. Signals partially disagree by design → the disagreement penalty lands it
   mid-range, which is the honest answer ("uncertain").

## Architecture (module map)

| File          | Responsibility                                             |
|---------------|------------------------------------------------------------|
| `app.py`      | Flask app, routes (`/submit`, `/appeal`, `/log`, `/health`), rate limiting |
| `signals.py`  | The three detection signals                                |
| `scoring.py`  | Combine signals → confidence; map confidence → label       |
| `db.py`       | SQLite content store + structured audit log                |

### API surface (contract)

- `POST /submit` — body `{text, creator_id}` → `{content_id, creator_id, attribution, confidence, signals{}, label{}, status, timestamp}`
- `POST /appeal` — body `{content_id, creator_reasoning}` → `{content_id, status, message, timestamp}`
- `GET /log?limit=N` — → `{entries: [...]}` (most recent audit entries)
- `GET /health` — liveness probe

## AI Tool Plan

**M3 — submission endpoint + first signal.**
Provide the AI tool: §1 (Detection signals, esp. Signal 1) + the architecture diagram +
the API contract. Ask it to generate the Flask skeleton with a `POST /submit` stub and the
`llm_signal(text)` function returning `{ai_likelihood, reliable, detail}`. Verify by calling
`llm_signal` directly on the sample human/AI paragraphs and checking the returned shape and
that scores move in the right direction *before* wiring it into the route.

**M4 — second/third signals + confidence scoring.**
Provide: §1 + §2 (uncertainty representation, incl. the exact combining formula and
thresholds) + the diagram. Ask for `structural_signal`, `lexical_signal`, and
`combine(...)` in `scoring.py`. Verify the generated `combine` matches the documented
weights, disagreement penalty, and the 0.30/0.70 thresholds (AI tools often invent
plausible-but-different math). Test with the 4 calibration inputs (clear AI, clear human,
formal human, edited AI) and confirm scores span the bands.

**M5 — production layer.**
Provide: §3 (label variants, verbatim) + §4 (appeals) + the diagram. Ask for
`make_label(confidence)` and the `POST /appeal` endpoint. Verify all three label variants
are reachable and their text matches §3 exactly, and that an appeal flips status to
`under_review` and writes a linked audit entry.
